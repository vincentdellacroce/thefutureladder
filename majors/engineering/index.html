⚙️ Engineering

"The bridge between science and the world."
What is Engineering?
Design. Build. Solve.

Engineering is the application of math, science, and creative thinking to design and build solutions to real-world problems. Engineers work on everything from microchips to skyscrapers, from medical devices to rocket engines. It is consistently one of the highest-earning undergraduate degrees available — and one of the most respected.

Engineering programs are rigorous. Expect calculus from day one, heavy lab work, team projects, and a demanding workload. But the payoff — intellectually, financially, and in terms of career flexibility — is enormous.

Key fact: Engineers can pivot into finance (as quantitative analysts), medicine (biomedical), law (patent attorney), business (product management), and policy. Few degrees give you more career optionality.

What Engineers Do Day-to-Day

  • Design systems, structures, or products using CAD software
  • Run simulations and analyze data to test feasibility
  • Collaborate with teams across disciplines
  • Write technical reports and present findings
  • Manage timelines, budgets, and project scope
  • Troubleshoot failures and iterate on designs

High School Blueprint: What to Focus On

  • 9th: Build your math foundation. Pre-Calc or Algebra 2 Honors. Join robotics or coding club.
  • 10th: AP Computer Science Principles (intro). Take Honors Chemistry. Start competing in Science Olympiad or robotics.
  • 11th: AP Calc BC, AP Physics C (Mech). Intern at a local engineering firm. Lead a team in your club.
  • 12th: AP Physics C (E&M), AP Chemistry. Complete a personal engineering project. Apply to BS/MS programs.

Key Skills to Develop

  • Advanced mathematics (calculus, linear algebra, diff eq)
  • Technical writing and documentation
  • CAD/Design tools (try Fusion 360 — it's free)
  • Coding (Python + MATLAB are the most used in engineering)
  • Systems thinking and problem decomposition
AP Courses
What to Take & When
Strategy: Engineering schools care most about AP Calc BC and AP Physics C. A 5 on both signals you can handle freshman engineering coursework. Don't skip these.

🔴 Essential (Take These)

AP Calc BC AP Physics C: Mechanics AP Physics C: E&M AP Chemistry AP Computer Science A
  • AP Calc BC — The backbone of all engineering. Master this. A 5 gives you college credit and skips into Calc II or III.
  • AP Physics C: Mechanics — The physics that actually matters for most engineering. Algebra is not enough — this is calculus-based.
  • AP Physics C: E&M — Critical for electrical and computer engineers. Very difficult — take after Mechanics.
  • AP Chemistry — Essential for chemical, materials, and biomedical engineers.
  • AP CS A — Java-based coding. Even non-software engineers code constantly.

🟡 Highly Recommended

AP Statistics AP Biology AP Environmental Science AP CS Principles
  • AP Statistics — Data analysis is used in every branch of engineering. This builds intuition.
  • AP Biology — Important for biomedical and environmental engineers.
  • AP CS Principles — Good intro before AP CS A if you're new to coding.

🟢 Good Additions

AP English Lang AP US History AP Macro/Micro

Engineers need to communicate. AP English Language trains technical writing and argumentation — both essential in professional engineering. Economics helps for engineers going into management or starting companies.

Extracurriculars
Build a Story, Not a List
The rule: Depth over breadth. One engineering EC where you built something, competed, and led beats five clubs where you just attended meetings.

🏆 Tier 1 — Most Impressive

  • FIRST Robotics (FRC) — The gold standard. Build a robot from scratch in 6 weeks. Colleges love it. Aim for a leadership role.
  • Science Olympiad — Broad STEM competition. Win at invitational, regional, state level. Demonstrates breadth and competitive drive.
  • Research internship — Work at a university lab or engineering firm. This is the highest signal possible. Cold email professors.
  • Intel/Regeneron ISEF — National science fair. A finalist spot is resume gold for every top engineering school.
  • USA(J)MO / AIME qualifier — Math excellence is a major green flag for engineering admissions.

🥈 Tier 2 — Excellent

  • Engineering club / President — Especially if you build real projects (bridges, circuits, software)
  • VEX Robotics — Slightly less prestigious than FRC but still excellent, more accessible
  • Physics Bowl / F=ma — Physics competitions signal you can handle the core material
  • Personal engineering project — Build something. Document it on GitHub or a website. Show it in your application.
  • Math Team / AMC 10/12 — Getting AIME-qualified is a meaningful achievement

🛠️ Personal Projects (Do These)

  • Build something with Arduino or Raspberry Pi
  • Design a bridge or structure and stress-test it
  • Code a functional app or tool (put it on GitHub)
  • Document your process — engineers are judged on documentation

  • Getting a PE license (Professional Engineer)

  • Location: SF, NYC, Seattle vs. mid-sized cities

Fields of Engineering
Which Branch Is Right for You?
⚡ Electrical Engineering
$78K → $145K (20 yrs)
Circuits, semiconductors, power systems, embedded systems. The backbone of every electronic device ever made. Highest job demand with tech sector growth. Pivots well into software/hardware roles at Apple, NVIDIA, Intel.
🔧 Mechanical Engineering
$72K → $130K (20 yrs)
The broadest engineering discipline. Thermal systems, fluids, dynamics, manufacturing. Works in automotive, aerospace, robotics, HVAC, biomedical. Strong CAD skills essential. Most flexible branch.
🏗️ Civil Engineering
$62K → $110K (20 yrs)
Infrastructure, bridges, roads, water systems, structural analysis. Essential for urban development. PE license is almost universal. Government and consulting are major employers. Steady, stable career.
⚗️ Chemical Engineering
$79K → $145K (20 yrs)
Materials, pharmaceuticals, energy, processing. Heavy overlap with chemistry and biology. Works at Dow, BASF, Exxon, Pfizer. One of the highest-paid starting salaries in engineering. Very math-heavy.
🚀 Aerospace Engineering
$75K → $138K (20 yrs)
Aircraft, spacecraft, propulsion, aerodynamics. Employers: Boeing, Lockheed, SpaceX, NASA, Northrop. Dream field for many but limited geographic flexibility (you'll likely move). Purdue, MIT, and Michigan dominate recruiting.
🧬 Biomedical Engineering
$65K → $120K (20 yrs)
Medical devices, prosthetics, imaging, drug delivery. Bridges engineering and medicine. Great pipeline into med school OR into companies like Medtronic, Boston Scientific, Abbott. Growing extremely fast.
🛢️ Petroleum Engineering
$100K → $185K (20 yrs)
The highest average starting salary of any engineering degree. Works with oil/gas extraction, well design, reservoir analysis. High pay, but industry is volatile and geographically concentrated (Texas, Middle East).
🤖 Robotics / Systems Engineering
$80K → $155K (20 yrs)
Mechatronics, control systems, automation. The fastest-growing field in engineering. Heavy demand from autonomous vehicles, manufacturing, defense. Requires fluency in both EE and ME — and always software.
🌿 Environmental Engineering
$58K → $105K (20 yrs)
Water/air quality, waste management, remediation. Works with the EPA, environmental consulting firms, municipalities. Lower pay ceiling but meaningful work. Often combined with civil engineering degrees.
⚛️ Nuclear Engineering
$76K → $145K (20 yrs)
Reactor design, radiation, nuclear medicine, energy. Major employers: NRC, national labs (Argonne, Oak Ridge), Navy nuclear program. Niche but extremely high demand and security. MIT, Penn State, Michigan lead programs.
🏭 Industrial Engineering
$68K → $125K (20 yrs)
Optimization, operations, logistics, supply chain. The "business engineering" — works inside companies to make them more efficient. Very high demand at Amazon, Tesla, Boeing, consulting firms. Great MBA pipeline.
🧱 Materials Science & Engineering
$68K → $125K (20 yrs)
Metals, polymers, semiconductors, nanomaterials. Works in R&D, manufacturing, electronics. Critical to battery tech, semiconductors, and aerospace. Often combined with physics or chemistry at the graduate level.
Resources
Best Materials for Engineering
📱 Websites & Platforms
Free Courseware
MIT OpenCourseWare
Free MIT courses including 18.01 (Calc), 8.01 (Physics), 2.001 (Mechanics of Materials). The real MIT curriculum, free.
Foundation
Khan Academy
Calculus, physics, chemistry — world-class explanations for every AP course. Use this to fill gaps.
YouTube
3Blue1Brown
Visual explanations of calculus and linear algebra that build deep intuition. Watch the Essence of Calculus series.
Online Courses
edX
MIT/Harvard/Berkeley courses online. Take actual university courses in high school. Huge resume signal.
Free Software
Autodesk Fusion 360
Free CAD/CAM software for students. Learn 3D modeling and design — used by actual engineers and NASA contractors.
YouTube
Engineering Explained
Mechanical/automotive engineering explained visually. Great for exploring what engineering actually looks like.
Coding
LeetCode
Essential for software/computer engineers. Start practicing now — tech company interviews are 100% algorithm-based.
Competition
FIRST Robotics (FRC)
Find and join a team near you. The most prestigious STEM extracurricular for engineering-bound students.
📚 Books
Book
The Design of Everyday Things — Don Norman
Essential reading for any engineer. Shows you how to think about design and usability. Referenced in college applications constantly.
Book
Engineering in Plain Sight — Grady Hillhouse
What all that infrastructure around you actually does and how it works. Eye-opening for aspiring civil and mechanical engineers.
Book
Structures — J.E. Gordon
The best intuitive introduction to how structures work. Readable and fascinating — you'll see the world differently.
Book
The Martian — Andy Weir
Fiction, but excellent real-world engineering thinking. Problem-solving under constraints. Easy read that reinforces engineering mindset.
